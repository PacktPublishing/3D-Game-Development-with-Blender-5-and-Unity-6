Combine the best of two powerful tools, Blender 5 and Unity 6, to create stunning, real-time 3D games. This hands-on guide shows solo developers, artists, and small studios on how to unify modeling, animation, and scripting into a streamlined workflow.
You’ll build a sci-fi first-person adventure from scratch, designing locations, props, characters, and animations in Blender, then importing and optimizing them in Unity. Learn how to use Geometry Nodes, the Asset Browser, and procedural modeling to speed up production. Automate asset exports and scene updates using Python and C#, and exchange non-geometric data like materials and animations across platforms.
The book also explores key topics like simulation, rigid bodies, and cinematic sequences. Whether you’re refining draft scenes, generating procedural worlds, or building final assets for Unity, you’ll gain the technical skills to work smarter and faster.
By the end, you'll have built an integrated pipeline and a playable game, with confidence to reuse the techniques across future projects.

<details open> 
  <summary><h2>Requirements for this book</summary>
This book is ideal for solo developers, technical artists, and small studios who want to unify
their creative and technical workflows using Blender and Unity. Whether you're a Unity user
looking to move away from costly 3D tools or a Blender artist eager to bring your creations to
life through gameplay, this book will walk you through every step of the production pipeline.
Production managers and visual artists interested in migrating their pipeline to Blender/Unity
would also benefit.

Readers are assumed to be familiar with how objects are moved, rotated, and scaled in Blender
and Unity, and to have a basic understanding of 3D in general, in terms of how mesh models
are structured in vertices, edges, faces, and objects. The book does its best to offer a brief
explanation of new concepts as they are introduced, but fundamental information like
navigating a folder structure is taken for granted. Elements of the user interface are addressed
with terms like Viewport, Panel, and Area, which are intuitive enough but better understood
with a basic knowledge of the software. Knowledge of common modeling operations like bevel
or extrusion definitely helps undestand the chapters on model generation.
Mac users are supposed to be aware that the Ctrl and Alt keys are equivalent, respectively, to
the Cmd and Option keys on their keyboards, and to figure out the minor differences between
the screenshots in the book and what they see on their monitors.
The software used in this book is limited to Unity 6.3, Blender 5.1, and occasionally a
programmer text editor, like Notepad++ for Windows or CotEditor for Mac. Blender 5.2 LTS,
which will be released a few months after the publication of this book, might be a good
replacement for Blender 5.1, which is the latest available at the time of writing.
  </details>
